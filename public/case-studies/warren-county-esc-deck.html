Pedagogical Framework: Adult Learning Design

Instructional delivery: 4 hours across 2 sessions | Trainer: Brandon Z. Hoff

Session 1: Foundations & Psychological Safety (2 hours)

Instructional Strategies

  • Normalized uncertainty: "92% of AI pilots are failing—you're not behind"
  • Started with "why": Khan Academy TED Talk before tool demonstrations
  • Data-driven baseline: Presented participants' own survey results
  • Accessible language: "I'm not a computer scientist" positioning
  • Hands-on modeling: Live demonstrations (voice features, image generation)

Participant Outcomes

  • Anxiety eliminated (12% → 0%)
  • "I understand how AI works" (conceptual foundation)
  • "I can use AI" (self-efficacy established)
  • Resistant participant shifted from "AI not appropriate" to "School and AI can work together"
  • Created appetite for application-focused learning

Session 2: Application & Skill Development (2 hours)

Instructional Strategies

  • Real-world demonstration: Converted participant evaluations to spreadsheet via OCR
  • Task-based learning: Teacher evaluations (OTES), calendar management
  • Explicit skill instruction: Prompt structure, iteration, refinement
  • Systems thinking: Introduced workflows, prompt libraries, automation
  • Differentiated delivery: Live Q&A addressed beginner to advanced needs

Participant Outcomes

  • "I can write OTES reports w/ease" (immediate application)
  • Practical focus increased to 70%
  • "I want a prompt library" (desire for systematization)
  • 50% sought advanced "how-to's" and depth
  • Requested hands-on automation sessions

Pedagogical Principle Applied: Andragogy (adult learning theory) prioritized immediate relevance, practical problem-solving, and experiential learning over theoretical knowledge transmission. Gradual release of responsibility (I Do → We Do → You Do) scaffolded skill development across sessions.

Critical Success Factors

Instructional Design

1. Prioritized Emotion Before Cognition

Addressed anxiety first (psychological safety), built self-efficacy second (competence), taught skills third (mastery). Normalized struggle and positioned instructor as fellow learner.

2. Made Learning Context-Relevant

Survey results centered participant experience. Every example drawn from actual work context (OTES evaluations, calendar management). "Prove to me I can save time" framing.

3. Balanced Foundation with Application

Session 1: Conceptual understanding (how AI works, Khan Academy video). Session 2: Operational skills (prompting, workflows, automation). Avoided premature skill instruction.

Learning Environment

4. Created Strategic Cognitive Dissonance

Demonstrated surprising capabilities (Jay Gatsby conversations, QR code generation, image creation) to spark curiosity without overwhelming. Juxtaposed assumptions with reality.

5. Converted Resistance Through Experience

Addressed opposition with education-specific examples (Khanmigo, Magic School AI) rather than argument. Participant who opposed AI discovered "School and AI can work together" through firsthand interaction.

6. Tracked Mindset, Not Just Skills

Four Quadrant Evaluations measured confidence, anxiety, and engagement—not just knowledge acquisition. Validated that emotional readiness preceded skill development.

Key Insight: The transformation was not about AI technology—it was about meeting adult learners at their emotional and cognitive readiness level, then scaffolding them systematically toward competence and confidence. Technical mastery was subordinated to psychological safety and immediate utility.

Implications for K-12 AI Adoption

Replicable Implementation Framework

Pre-Training Requirements

  • Baseline assessment via survey instrument
  • Map results to competency framework (e.g., RUDI)
  • Identify primary concerns and motivations
  • Segment cohort by readiness level

Session 1 Design Principles

  • Start with "why" not "what" (vision before tools)
  • Normalize uncertainty and struggle
  • Use accessible, non-technical language
  • Demonstrate features hands-on
  • Address resistance through experience

Session 2 Design Principles

  • Open with immediate, relevant utility
  • Organize around tasks, not tools
  • Teach skills explicitly (prompting, iteration)
  • Introduce systems thinking (workflows, libraries)
  • Differentiate for multiple skill levels

Ongoing Development Strategy

  • Move 50% to Intermediate operational proficiency
  • Develop peer mentorship structures
  • Establish district governance framework
  • Create prompt library infrastructure
  • Integrate student AI literacy curriculum

Measurement Approach

  • Track mindset shifts (anxiety, confidence, engagement)
  • Measure skill progression (RUDI framework levels)
  • Document use cases and time savings
  • Monitor governance compliance
  • Assess organizational culture change

Scale Considerations

  • Executive team as change agents (train-the-trainer)
  • Differentiated pathways for varied readiness
  • Asynchronous resources for self-paced learning
  • Integration with existing professional development

Transferability Assessment: This approach is replicable for mid-sized districts (1,000-5,000 employees) with executive teams of 20-40 members. Required investment: 4-8 hours initial training, ongoing support infrastructure, and organizational commitment to governance development.

Conclusion

Warren County School District demonstrates that rapid, measurable AI literacy development is achievable when training prioritizes adult learning principles over technical content delivery. In 8 weeks and 4 instructional hours, the executive leadership team progressed from Pre-Beginner awareness to Beginner/Low-Intermediate operational readiness.

Key Takeaways

What Worked

  • Psychological safety preceded skill instruction
  • Immediate utility demonstrated before theory
  • Accessible language reduced cognitive barriers
  • Task-based learning anchored to real work
  • Resistance addressed through experience, not argument
  • Differentiation accommodated varied readiness

Measurable Outcomes

  • Anxiety eliminated (12% → 0%)
  • Practical focus increased 250% (20% → 70%)
  • Depth-seeking emerged (50% wanted advanced training)
  • Conceptual understanding established
  • Operational skills development initiated
  • Desire for systematization created (workflows, libraries)

"This is not a story of technology adoption. This is a story of adult learners moving from fear to fluency—and that journey is replicable."

The Warren County case validates that educator AI competency development requires pedagogical expertise, not just technical knowledge. Districts seeking to accelerate AI literacy should invest in instructional design that addresses emotional readiness, contextual relevance, and systematic skill progression.

This model demonstrates that AI literacy training, when designed through adult learning principles, can scale across districts with minimal time investment and measurable cultural impact.
